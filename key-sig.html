Key Signatures

We need key signatures to be able to tell which notes we're playing. By having key signatures it also makes it easier to look at and to write.

There are a total of 15 key signature which we call scales. 7 of them are flat and 7 of them are natrual(shaps). there are 2 scales that have diffrent names but are the same and one that has no sharps or flats.

Basics

All of the scales add a flat or sharp. The best way to remember the order of the flats and sharps is the acronym BEAD Greatest Common Factor In flats we add in this exact order but with sharps we add going backwards.

Every instrument will start on a different note but keep in mind the name and order of the scales never change. If you're stuggling to remember what note is flat or sharp look at what line it's on and that's the note that we play flat or sharp.

Flats

The symbol for flats almost looks like a mini lowercase b. The order we add flats is BEADGCF.

When you start with the names of the flats we start on C then F. After that we start at the beginning and end at G

The best way to remember what the name of the scale is is to look back at the acronym and go one letter before the note you're adding. Let's take the A flat scale for example which adds D flat. On the acronym A comes before D and that's the name of the scale!

The ones that may not exactly follow this theory is the scale F witch adds B flat. In that case we go back to the beginning of the acronym which is B.

Sharps

The middle line that's between the 2 dots is the note F. The best way to remember this is that the bass cleff is in the key of F hence the way the bass cleff symbol is. After that it starts the normal order G, A, B, C, etc. on the lines, then repeats.

The best way to remember the lines starting from the bottom is the acronym Great Big Dogs Fight Animals. The best way to remember the spaces is the acronym All Cows Eat Grass.

Instruments that use Bass

Lower pitch horns, strings, and pianos
